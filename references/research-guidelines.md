# Research Quality Guidelines

## Source Quality Standards

### Primary Sources (Strongly Preferred)
- Official company website: About, Careers, Products, Press, Investor Relations, Engineering Blog
- Product documentation, help centers, changelogs, and release notes
- Official company blog posts and announcements
- SEC filings, S-1s, and investor materials
- Verified company social media accounts

### Executive Intelligence Sources (PM-Critical)
- Podcast interviews: Lenny's Podcast, Masters of Scale, How I Built This, All-In, acquired.fm, Invest Like the Best, SaaStr podcast
- Conference keynotes and talks: SaaStr, ProductCon, Mind the Product, AWS re:Invent, Google I/O, Stripe Sessions
- Earnings call transcripts (Seeking Alpha, The Motley Fool, company IR pages)
- Long-form press profiles: The Information, Forbes, Fortune, Bloomberg Businessweek, Wired
- Published essays or threads from executives on LinkedIn or Substack

### PM Intelligence Sources (PM-Critical)
- Glassdoor: filter for PM-specific reviews (not just overall company reviews)
- Blind: PM channel threads
- LinkedIn job descriptions for PM roles — read these carefully for skills valued, team structure signals, and seniority expectations
- PM interview prep communities (e.g., Exponent, PM Exercises, Reddit r/ProductManagement)
- Medium or Substack posts by current or former employees

### Acceptable Secondary Sources
- Major tech press: TechCrunch, The Verge, Wired, Fast Company, Business Insider
- Industry analyst reports: Gartner, Forrester, IDC
- App store listings (Apple App Store, Google Play) for features and user reviews
- G2, Capterra, Trustpilot for product sentiment
- Crunchbase, PitchBook, LinkedIn for funding and headcount
- Product Hunt launch pages and comments

### Sources to Avoid
- Unverified blogs without clear authorship
- AI-generated summaries of companies (circular sourcing risk)
- Wikipedia (acceptable for quick orientation only — never cite directly)
- Sponsored content or advertorials
- Reddit posts for factual claims (acceptable for sentiment only)
- Sources older than 24 months for strategic or market claims

## Citation Standards

### Format
Use this format: `[Site Name | Page Title](url) — accessed [date]`

**Examples:**
- `[Lenny's Podcast | How Linear builds product](https://www.lennyspodcast.com/...) — accessed 2025-11`
- `[Glassdoor | Linear PM Reviews](https://glassdoor.com/...) — accessed 2025-11`
- `[TechCrunch | Figma raises $200M](https://techcrunch.com/...) — accessed 2025-09`

### Inline Citations
- Cite immediately after the claim
- One citation per distinct claim or related set of facts
- When multiple sources support the same claim, cite the strongest

### Executive Quotes
- Always include: who said it, source name, and date
- Format: *"[Quote]"* — [Name], [Title], [Source], [Date]
- Never paraphrase a quote as if it were verbatim

## Research Process Guidelines

### Depth vs. Breadth
- Quality over quantity: 10 strong sources beat 20 weak ones
- For complex products or companies, 15–20 sources may be needed
- Stop when you have comprehensive, verified answers for all relevant sections
- Don't pad source lists — every source should be cited

### Multi-Source Verification
- For key claims (revenue, headcount, major milestones), verify across 2+ sources
- Flag conflicting information between sources
- Default to official company sources when discrepancies exist
- For executive quotes, always trace back to the original interview or transcript

### Temporal Awareness
- Mark all time-sensitive data with "As of [date]"
- Prioritize sources from the last 12 months for strategic, market, and leadership claims
- Historical facts (founding, pivots, acquisitions) can use older sources — still note date
- If a relevant source is 18–24 months old, note it may be outdated

## Handling Missing Information

### When Official Info is Limited
1. Search for press coverage or analyst reports
2. Check app stores or product directories for product details
3. Look for executive interviews that address the topic
4. Review PM job descriptions for organizational signals
5. If genuinely unavailable: state explicitly — "Not publicly available as of [date]"

### What Not to Do
- Do not speculate or infer facts without evidence
- Do not use vague language like "reportedly" or "various sources indicate" without actual citations
- Do not fill gaps with generic industry statements
- Do not assume that absence of negative signals means the company is healthy — look actively for challenges

## Section-Specific Guidelines

### Executive Insights
- Prioritize interviews over press releases — executives are more candid in long-form conversation
- Look for what they believe that others don't — this is more revealing than consensus takes
- Note when an executive quote is from earnings (often more guarded) vs. a podcast (often candid)
- Earnings calls: listen for language about "challenges," "headwinds," or "areas of investment" — these reveal priorities even when framed diplomatically

### PM Org & Culture
- Read PM job descriptions from the last 6 months — they reveal what skills are valued right now
- On Glassdoor, filter to PM roles specifically; general reviews don't capture the PM experience
- Look for patterns across multiple PM reviews, not individual outliers
- High PM autonomy signals: "you own the roadmap," "no PMs as project managers," "strong product culture"
- Low PM autonomy signals: "PM as coordinator," heavy engineering-led culture, lack of senior PM leadership visibility

### Transformation & Innovation Strategy
- Look for acquisition announcements (often in press releases and TechCrunch)
- Check LinkedIn for recent senior hires — a new VP of AI or Head of Data signals a strategic shift
- Engineering blog posts often telegraph strategic priorities 6–12 months before they're announced publicly
- Earnings calls and investor letters are the most reliable source for stated strategic priorities

### Competitive Positioning
- Look for where the company explicitly positions against competitors (landing pages, comparison pages, sales decks if leaked)
- G2 and Capterra "compare" pages show how users think about alternatives
- Press coverage of customer switches (churn to or from a competitor) is valuable signal
- Avoid reporting competitive claims from the company's own marketing — verify independently

### Strategic Signals
- Distinguish between what a company says it does and what the evidence shows it does
- Look for behavioral evidence of practices: shipping cadence, public A/B test disclosures, data blog posts
- Industry-shaping moves often appear first in obscure conference talks or engineering blog posts before becoming mainstream
- Mark all synthesis and inference explicitly — separate observed facts from drawn conclusions
